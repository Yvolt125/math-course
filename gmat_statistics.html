Lesson 1

Mean, Median & Mode

Objectives
  • Calculate the arithmetic mean (average) as the sum divided by the count.
  • Find the median by ordering a data set — it is the middle value (or average of the two middle values).
  • Identify when the mean and median differ, and what that reveals about the data's distribution.
📌 Core Concept

Mean = Sum of values ÷ Count. Median = the middle value when data is sorted in order. Mode = the most frequently occurring value.

The GMAT frequently gives you the average and count, then asks for the sum — or gives the sum and count, then asks you to add a value that changes the average. The relationship Sum = Mean × Count is the key tool.

How to Approach It
  1. 1Use Sum = Mean × Count to find a missing sum or to work backwards from a target average.
  2. 2For median questions: sort the data first, then locate the middle position(s). For n values, the median is at position (n+1)/2.
  3. 3For "new average after adding a value" questions: compute the old sum, add the new value, divide by new count.
  4. 4Note whether the question asks for mean, median, or mode — they can all give different answers for the same data set.
Example

The average (mean) of five numbers is 14. When a sixth number is added, the new average becomes 15. What is the sixth number?

(A) 15(B) 18(C) 20(D) 22(E) 25
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). Old sum = 5 × 14 = 70. New sum = 6 × 15 = 90. Sixth number = 90 − 70 = 20. Common trap: choosing (A) 15 because "the new average is 15" — the sixth number must be higher than the new average to pull it up.

Common Traps
  • Adding a new value equal to the current average and expecting the average to change — adding a value equal to the current mean leaves the mean unchanged.
  • Forgetting to sort the data before finding the median — the median is positional, not the arithmetical middle of the range.
Key Takeaways
  • Sum = Mean × Count. Use this to find any one of the three quantities given the other two.
  • Median requires sorting first. For an even number of values, average the two middle numbers.
  • Mode is simply the most frequent value — a data set can have no mode, one mode, or multiple modes.
💡 Quick Tip

Whenever the GMAT gives you an average, immediately multiply by the count to get the sum. This one step unlocks almost every "average" question on the test.

Lesson 2

Weighted Averages

Objectives
  • Compute a weighted average as (Σ weight × value) ÷ Σ weights.
  • Recognise that the overall average is pulled toward the group with the larger weight (count).
  • Use the alligation shortcut to quickly estimate weighted averages in mixture-style problems.
📌 Core Concept

A weighted average accounts for the fact that different values contribute differently based on their frequency or proportion. If one group is much larger than another, the combined average is pulled toward that group's value — never simply the arithmetic mean of the two group averages.

Weighted average = (n₁ × avg₁ + n₂ × avg₂) ÷ (n₁ + n₂).

How to Approach It
  1. 1Compute the total sum for each group: sum = count × average.
  2. 2Add the sums across all groups to get the grand total sum.
  3. 3Divide the grand total sum by the total count across all groups.
  4. 4Sanity-check: the result must lie between the two group averages (never outside that range).
Example

Class A has 20 students with an average score of 80. Class B has 30 students with an average score of 70. What is the combined average score of all students?

(A) 73(B) 74(C) 75(D) 76(E) 77
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (B). Sum A = 20 × 80 = 1,600. Sum B = 30 × 70 = 2,100. Total sum = 3,700. Total students = 50. Average = 3,700 ÷ 50 = 74. Common trap: averaging 80 and 70 to get 75 — this only works if both groups are the same size. Class B is larger, so the answer is pulled toward 70.

Common Traps
  • Simple-averaging two group averages when the group sizes are unequal — always weight by count (or frequency).
  • Expecting the combined average to equal the midpoint — it only does when both groups are the same size.
Key Takeaways
  • Weighted average = (Σ n × avg) ÷ Σ n. Each group's average is weighted by its count.
  • The result is always between the two group averages and is pulled toward the larger group.
  • Simple arithmetic average of group averages is only valid when groups are equal-sized.
💡 Quick Tip

The combined average is always closer to the average of the larger group. Use this as a quick check: if you average two groups and your answer is closer to the smaller group, something went wrong.

Lesson 3

Range & Standard Deviation

Objectives
  • Calculate the range as max − min and understand what it measures.
  • Understand standard deviation as a measure of spread around the mean — without computing it by hand.
  • Compare the standard deviations of two sets by reasoning about their spread, not by calculating.
📌 Core Concept

Range = largest value − smallest value. Simple to compute but sensitive to outliers.

Standard deviation (SD) measures how spread out values are around the mean. A higher SD means values are more scattered; a lower SD means they cluster tightly around the mean. On the GMAT, you are tested on the concept of standard deviation — never asked to compute it by hand.

Adding or subtracting the same constant to every value does not change the SD (the spread stays identical). Multiplying every value by a constant scales the SD by that constant.

How to Approach It
  1. 1For range questions: identify max and min, then subtract.
  2. 2For SD comparison questions: visualise how tightly clustered the values are around the mean — closer clustering means lower SD.
  3. 3If every value in a set is shifted by the same amount (±k), the mean changes but SD stays the same.
  4. 4If every value is multiplied by k, the SD is also multiplied by k (and the mean is multiplied by k).
Example

Set A = {2, 4, 6, 8, 10} and Set B = {4, 5, 6, 7, 8}. Which set has the greater standard deviation?

(A) Set A(B) Set B(C) They are equal(D) Cannot be determined
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (A). Both sets have mean = 6. Set A values range from 2 to 10 — each is further from the mean. Set B values range from 4 to 8 — they cluster more tightly around 6. No calculation needed: more spread = higher SD. Set A has a greater standard deviation.

Common Traps
  • Thinking that adding a constant to every value increases the standard deviation — shift operations change the mean, not the spread.
  • Confusing range with standard deviation — a set can have a large range but still have most values clustered near the mean, giving a moderate SD.
Key Takeaways
  • Range = max − min. Quick to compute; sensitive to extreme values.
  • Standard deviation measures spread around the mean. Compare sets visually — no formula needed.
  • Shifting all values by ±k: mean changes, SD unchanged. Multiplying by k: both mean and SD scale by k.
💡 Quick Tip

For standard deviation comparison questions, find both means, then ask: "Which set's values deviate more from their mean?" That set has the higher SD. You do not need to compute SD — just reason about spread.

Lesson 4

Overlapping Sets (Venn Diagrams)

Objectives
  • Apply the inclusion-exclusion principle: |A ∪ B| = |A| + |B| − |A ∩ B|.
  • Set up a 2×2 matrix or Venn diagram to organise two-category problems.
  • Solve three-category overlapping set problems using the extended inclusion-exclusion formula.
📌 Core Concept

When counting elements that belong to one or more categories, simply adding each group's count double-counts items in the overlap. The inclusion-exclusion principle corrects for this: Total in A or B = |A| + |B| − |A ∩ B|.

For GMAT purposes, a 2×2 table (rows = in/out of A; columns = in/out of B) is usually faster and less error-prone than a Venn diagram for two-category problems.

How to Approach It
  1. 1Draw a 2×2 table with category A (yes/no) as rows and category B (yes/no) as columns.
  2. 2Fill in every cell you can from the given information.
  3. 3Use row/column totals to find the missing cell.
  4. 4Verify: all row totals and column totals should sum to the grand total.
Example

Of 100 students, 60 study French, 50 study Spanish, and 20 study both. How many students study neither French nor Spanish?

(A) 5(B) 10(C) 20(D) 30(E) 40
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (B). Students studying at least one language = |F| + |S| − |F ∩ S| = 60 + 50 − 20 = 90. Neither = 100 − 90 = 10. Common trap: adding 60 + 50 = 110 and forgetting to subtract the overlap of 20 — this overestimates students studying at least one language.

Common Traps
  • Adding all group sizes without subtracting the overlap — this double-counts students in both categories.
  • Confusing "only French" (French but not Spanish = 60 − 20 = 40) with "French" (all 60, including those also taking Spanish).
Key Takeaways
  • |A ∪ B| = |A| + |B| − |A ∩ B|. Always subtract the overlap once.
  • Neither = Total − |A ∪ B|.
  • A 2×2 matrix keeps the four regions organised: only A, only B, both, neither.
💡 Quick Tip

Draw the 2×2 table before writing any equation. Label rows "French / Not French" and columns "Spanish / Not Spanish." Fill in what you know, then the unknown cell falls out from the row or column total.
