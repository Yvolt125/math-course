Lesson 1

2-Statement Logic

Objectives
  • Memorise and apply the five fixed GMAT Data Sufficiency answer choices to any DS question.
  • Evaluate each statement independently before considering them together — never import information from one statement into your evaluation of the other.
  • Work through the AD/BCE decision tree efficiently to eliminate answer choices.
📌 Core Concept

Every DS question gives a question stem and two statements labeled (1) and (2). You never actually solve for a specific value — you determine whether you could solve it with the given information.

The task is not arithmetic; it is logical: does the information provided uniquely determine the answer, yes or no?

Mastering the five answer choices and applying them systematically turns DS into one of the most learnable question types on the GMAT.

How to Approach It
  1. 1Consider statement (1) alone — is it sufficient on its own to answer the question stem with certainty?
  2. 2Consider statement (2) alone — is it sufficient on its own, independent of statement (1)?
  3. 3If neither alone is sufficient, consider both statements together to see if they combine to give one definitive answer.
  4. 4Memorize the answer choices: A = (1) alone; B = (2) alone; C = both together; D = each alone; E = neither.
Example

Is integer n divisible by 6?
(1) n is divisible by 3.
(2) n is divisible by 2.

(A) Statement (1) alone is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient. (B) Statement (2) alone is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient. (C) Both statements together are sufficient, but neither alone is sufficient. (D) Each statement alone is sufficient. (E) Statements (1) and (2) together are not sufficient.
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). Statement (1) alone: n = 3 (not divisible by 6) or n = 6 (is divisible by 6) — not sufficient. Statement (2) alone: n = 2 (not divisible by 6) or n = 6 (is divisible by 6) — not sufficient. Together: if n is divisible by both 2 and 3, it is divisible by 6 (since 2 and 3 are co-prime). Sufficient. Common trap: choosing A or B because divisibility by 3 (or 2) feels "close enough" — but neither alone eliminates counterexamples.

Common Traps
  • Reading information from Statement 1 into your evaluation of Statement 2 — each must be evaluated in complete isolation.
  • Rushing to answer C (both together) without first fully checking whether A or B alone works — A or B alone is often sufficient when test-takers prematurely go to C.
Summary
  • The five choices always mean the same thing: A=(1) alone; B=(2) alone; C=both together; D=each alone; E=neither. Memorise them.
  • AD/BCE tree: if (1) alone is sufficient → check (2) alone → answer is A or D. If (1) alone is not sufficient → check (2) alone → answer is B, C, or E.
  • In DS, you are testing sufficiency, not computing an answer.
💡 Quick Tip

The five answer choices are always the same on every DS question — memorize them before test day. Once they are automatic, you free up working memory for the actual logic of the problem.

Lesson 2

Sufficiency vs Solvability

Objectives
  • Distinguish between "sufficient" (gives exactly one answer) and "solvable" (gives at least one answer).
  • Recognise that a definitive "No" is just as sufficient as a definitive "Yes."
  • Identify when a statement appears helpful but allows multiple values, making it insufficient.
📌 Core Concept

"Sufficient" means the statement gives exactly one answer — whether that answer is yes, no, or a specific number. The quality of the answer does not matter; its uniqueness does.

Saying "it depends" always means not sufficient. If two different scenarios are both consistent with the statement but yield different answers, the statement fails the sufficiency test.

Many students confuse being able to calculate something with being sufficient — but sufficiency is about uniqueness, not computability.

How to Approach It
  1. 1Don't solve — ask yourself: "Does this statement always produce one definitive answer, no matter what?"
  2. 2Beware of cases where a statement seems helpful but still allows multiple values — these are traps.
  3. 3Test edge cases deliberately: integers vs. non-integers, positive vs. negative, zero as a special case.
Example

What is the value of x?
(1) x² = 25
(2) x > 0

(A) Statement (1) alone is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient. (B) Statement (2) alone is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient. (C) Both statements together are sufficient, but neither alone is sufficient. (D) Each statement alone is sufficient. (E) Statements (1) and (2) together are not sufficient.
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). Statement (1) alone: x² = 25 → x = 5 or x = −5. Two possible values → not sufficient. Statement (2) alone: x > 0 tells us the sign but not the value → not sufficient. Together: x² = 25 and x > 0 → x = 5 uniquely. Sufficient. Common trap: choosing A because "we can calculate x from x² = 25" — but calculating two values is not the same as uniquely determining one.

Common Traps
  • Concluding a statement is sufficient because it produces at least one valid answer — sufficiency requires exactly one answer, not just one possible answer.
  • In yes/no questions, concluding "insufficient" when a statement always gives "No" — a consistent "No" is sufficient.
Summary
  • Sufficient = the statement yields one and only one answer (a unique value, or always Yes, or always No).
  • Test edge cases: integers vs. fractions, positive vs. negative, zero. If any case gives a different answer, the statement is not sufficient.
  • "Could be" is not sufficient; "must be" is sufficient.
💡 Quick Tip

A definitive "No" is just as sufficient as a definitive "Yes" — sufficiency is about uniqueness, not about getting a nice answer. If the statement always gives you "No," that is sufficient.
