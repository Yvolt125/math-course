Lesson 1

Rates — Work & Speed

Objectives
  • Apply Rate × Time = Work/Distance to work and distance problems.
  • Combine rates correctly when workers or vehicles work simultaneously.
  • Calculate average speed as total distance ÷ total time — never as the average of two speeds.
📌 Core Concept

Rate problems involve the relationship Rate × Time = Work (or Distance). The key is identifying what "1 unit" of work or distance represents. Once rates are expressed in the same units, they can be added or compared directly.

When two workers work together simultaneously, their combined rate equals the sum of their individual rates: r_total = r₁ + r₂.

How to Approach It
  1. 1Define your variables: rate (r), time (t), and output — work or distance (w or d = r × t).
  2. 2For combined work: the total rate = r₁ + r₂, because rates add when workers work simultaneously.
  3. 3For speed problems: use d = rt. For average speed, always use total distance ÷ total time — never average the speeds directly.
  4. 4For "how long together" questions: set up (r₁ + r₂) × t = 1 job and solve for t.
Example

Machine A can complete a job in 4 hours. Machine B can complete the same job in 6 hours. If both machines work together, how long will it take them to complete the job?

(A) 2 hours(B) 2 hours 24 minutes(C) 3 hours(D) 5 hours(E) 10 hours
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (B). Rate A = 1/4 job/hr; Rate B = 1/6 job/hr. Combined rate = 1/4 + 1/6 = 3/12 + 2/12 = 5/12. Time = 1 ÷ (5/12) = 12/5 = 2.4 hours = 2 hours 24 minutes. Common trap: averaging the times (5 hours) instead of adding the rates.

Common Traps
  • Averaging speeds directly — this is only valid when times at each speed are equal. Always use total distance ÷ total time.
  • Forgetting to add rates for simultaneous work — if A and B work together, their combined rate is r_A + r_B, not an average.
Summary
  • Rate × Time = Work (or Distance). Rates add when workers work together.
  • Average speed = total distance / total time — never the arithmetic mean of speeds.
  • For combined-work time: (r₁ + r₂) × t = 1 job, then solve for t.
💡 Quick Tip

Average speed does not equal the arithmetic mean of two speeds unless the times spent at each speed are equal. Use total distance ÷ total time every time.

Lesson 2

Mixtures

Objectives
  • Set up the mixture equation: amount₁ × value₁ + amount₂ × value₂ = total amount × blended value.
  • Verify that the blended result falls between the two component values.
  • Use a table (Amount | Concentration | Total) to organise mixture problems before writing any equation.
📌 Core Concept

Mixture problems combine two substances with different concentrations or costs to form a new blend. The fundamental equation balances the contribution of each component to the total mixture value.

The blended value always falls between the two component values — this is a built-in sanity check for your answer.

How to Approach It
  1. 1Set up the mixture equation: (amount₁)(value₁) + (amount₂)(value₂) = (total amount)(blended value).
  2. 2Assign a variable for the unknown quantity (often one of the amounts).
  3. 3Solve the resulting linear equation and verify the answer makes physical sense (amounts must be positive).
  4. 4Check that the blended value falls between the two component values — if not, recheck your setup.
Example

A chemist mixes a 20% acid solution with a 50% acid solution to produce 20 litres of a 30% acid solution. How many litres of the 20% solution does she use?

(A) 6(B) 8(C) 12(D) 14(E) 16
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). Let x = litres of 20% solution; then (20 − x) litres of 50% solution. Mixture equation: 0.20x + 0.50(20 − x) = 0.30 × 20 → 0.20x + 10 − 0.50x = 6 → −0.30x = −4 → x = 12. Sanity check: 30% is closer to 20% than to 50%, so we need more of the 20% solution. ✓

Common Traps
  • Forgetting that the blended value must fall between the two component values — if your answer is outside this range, your equation is wrong.
  • Mixing up amount and concentration in the equation — always track units carefully and use a 3-column table.
Summary
  • Mixture equation: a₁v₁ + a₂v₂ = (a₁ + a₂) × v_blend.
  • Blended value is always between the two component values — use this as a check.
  • Organise with a 3-column table (Amount | Concentration/Cost | Total) before writing equations.
💡 Quick Tip

Draw a table with three columns — Amount | Concentration/Cost | Total — before writing any equation. This visual structure prevents the most common mixture setup errors.

Lesson 3

Interest

Objectives
  • Apply the simple interest formula I = PRT and distinguish it from compound interest.
  • Understand that compound interest yields more than simple interest because earned interest is reinvested.
  • Identify whether a question asks for interest earned or the total account balance.
📌 Core Concept

Simple interest uses I = PRT (Principal × Rate × Time). Compound interest grows the principal each period by reinvesting the earned interest. The GMAT tests your understanding of these concepts rather than complex arithmetic.

The critical distinction is that compound interest earns interest on previously earned interest, making it always greater than simple interest for the same rate and term.

How to Approach It
  1. 1For simple interest: I = P × r × t; Total amount = P + I.
  2. 2For compound interest: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where n is the number of compounding periods per year.
  3. 3Identify whether the question asks for interest earned or the total (principal + interest) — these are different values.
  4. 4For compound vs. simple comparisons, remember compound always yields more because interest earns interest.
Example

An investment of $800 earns simple interest at an annual rate of 5%. After how many years will the total value of the investment be $1,000?

(A) 2(B) 4(C) 5(D) 8(E) 10
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). Interest needed = $1,000 − $800 = $200. Using I = PRT: $200 = $800 × 0.05 × t → $200 = $40t → t = 5 years. Common trap: solving for interest earned ($200) and selecting (A) 2 or confusing it with the rate.

Common Traps
  • Confusing interest earned with total account balance — the question may ask for only one of these; re-read the question stem.
  • Applying simple interest when the problem says "compounded" — compound always means interest on interest, yielding more than simple.
Summary
  • Simple interest: I = P × r × t. Total = P + I.
  • Compound interest: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt). Always greater than simple for the same rate.
  • On GMAT, conceptual understanding of compound vs. simple matters more than exact computation.
💡 Quick Tip

For compound interest on the GMAT, a quick approximation is usually sufficient. The test is checking that you understand the concept — not that you can compute (1.06)^12 by hand.

Lesson 4

Translating Text → Equations

Objectives
  • Systematically translate English phrases into algebraic expressions using a fixed vocabulary (is=, more than=+, less than=−, of=×, per=÷).
  • Assign variables before writing any equation.
  • Re-read what the question is actually asking — it may request a derived value, not the raw variable.
📌 Core Concept

Word problems are algebra problems in disguise. The core skill is converting English phrases into precise mathematical expressions. A systematic translation process eliminates errors caused by misreading the problem.

The most important step comes last: re-read the question to confirm you are solving for what was actually asked — not just the variable you defined.

How to Approach It
  1. 1Identify the unknown and assign it a variable before doing anything else.
  2. 2Translate key phrases: "is" = =; "more than" = +; "less than" = −; "of" = ×; "per" = ÷; "ratio of a to b" = a/b.
  3. 3Write the complete equation, then solve and check that the answer satisfies the original word problem.
  4. 4Re-read the final question: it may ask for a derived value (e.g., x + 3), not x itself.
Example

Five less than three times a number equals seven more than the number. What is the number?

(A) 3(B) 6(C) 7(D) 11(E) 16
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (B). Let n = the number. "Three times a number" = 3n. "Five less than 3n" = 3n − 5. "Seven more than the number" = n + 7. Equation: 3n − 5 = n + 7 → 2n = 12 → n = 6. Common trap: translating "five less than three times a number" as 5 − 3n instead of 3n − 5.

Common Traps
  • "x less than y" means y − x, not x − y — English word order often reverses in math for subtraction. Always write out subtraction phrases carefully.
  • Solving for the variable defined but not answering the actual question — re-read the final question before circling your answer.
Summary
  • Define the unknown first, then translate phrase by phrase.
  • Key translations: "is"=, "more than"=+, "less than"=−, "of"=×, "per"=÷.
  • The final question asks for a specific value — make sure you report the right quantity, not just the variable you solved for.
💡 Quick Tip

"x less than y" means y − x, not x − y. The word order in English often reverses in math. Always slow down on subtraction phrases and write them out carefully.
