Lesson 1

Lines, Angles & Triangles

Objectives
  • Apply angle rules: supplementary (180°), vertical (equal), parallel lines cut by a transversal (alternate interior, co-interior).
  • Use the triangle angle sum (180°) and the Pythagorean theorem (a² + b² = c²).
  • Recognise and apply the 30-60-90 and 45-45-90 special right triangle ratios.
📌 Core Concept

The interior angles of any triangle sum to 180°. The exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles.

For right triangles: a² + b² = c² (Pythagorean theorem). Special triangles save time: 30-60-90 has sides in ratio 1 : √3 : 2. 45-45-90 has sides in ratio 1 : 1 : √2. Recognise the common Pythagorean triples: 3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17.

Key Formulas:
Triangle area = ½ × base × height
Pythagorean theorem: a² + b² = c² (c = hypotenuse)
30-60-90: sides = x, x√3, 2x
45-45-90: sides = x, x, x√2
How to Approach It
  1. 1Label all known angles and identify which angle rules apply (vertical angles, supplementary, parallel lines).
  2. 2Use the triangle angle sum (180°) to find a missing angle whenever two angles are known.
  3. 3Check whether a right triangle is a Pythagorean triple or a special triangle before applying the full formula.
  4. 4For area: identify the base and the corresponding perpendicular height — they must be at right angles.
Example

In right triangle ABC, angle C = 90° and the legs are AC = 5 and BC = 12. What is the length of the hypotenuse AB?

(A) 10(B) 11(C) 13(D) 15(E) 17
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). Recognise the 5-12-13 Pythagorean triple — no calculation needed. AB = 13. If you don't recall the triple: 5² + 12² = 25 + 144 = 169 = 13². Common trap: computing 5 + 12 = 17, selecting (E) — you must use the Pythagorean theorem, not simply add the legs.

Common Traps
  • Adding leg lengths to find the hypotenuse — always use a² + b² = c², never a + b = c.
  • Using a non-perpendicular height in the area formula — the height must be perpendicular to the base, not just any side of the triangle.
Key Takeaways
  • Triangle angles sum to 180°. Exterior angle = sum of two non-adjacent interior angles.
  • Pythagorean triples to memorise: 3-4-5, 5-12-13. (Multiples work too: 6-8-10, 9-12-15.)
  • Special right triangles: 45-45-90 (x, x, x√2); 30-60-90 (x, x√3, 2x).
💡 Quick Tip

Memorise the Pythagorean triples (3-4-5 and 5-12-13) and their multiples. On the GMAT, right triangles are almost always one of these triples, saving the time of computing √(a²+b²).

Lesson 2

Circles

Objectives
  • Apply the circle formulas: circumference = 2πr, area = πr².
  • Calculate arc length and sector area as a fraction of the full circle using the central angle.
  • Apply the inscribed angle theorem: an inscribed angle is half the central angle that subtends the same arc.
📌 Core Concept

For any circle with radius r: circumference = 2πr, area = πr². Diameter = 2r.

Arc length and sector area scale proportionally with the central angle: Arc length = (θ/360°) × 2πr. Sector area = (θ/360°) × πr². Think of them as fractions of the whole circle.

Key Formulas:
Circumference = 2πr = πd
Area = πr²
Arc length = (θ/360) × 2πr
Sector area = (θ/360) × πr²
Inscribed angle = ½ × (intercepted arc in degrees)
How to Approach It
  1. 1Identify whether you need the full circle (area/circumference) or a partial circle (arc/sector).
  2. 2For arc/sector: set up the ratio (central angle / 360°) and multiply by the full-circle formula.
  3. 3For inscribed angles: the angle equals half the central angle subtending the same arc.
  4. 4If the diameter is given instead of radius, halve it first: r = d/2.
Example

A circle has radius 6. What is the length of an arc subtended by a central angle of 60°?

(A) π(B) 2π(C) 3π(D) 4π(E) 6π
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (B). Full circumference = 2π × 6 = 12π. Arc = (60/360) × 12π = (1/6) × 12π = 2π. Common trap: using πr² (area formula) instead of 2πr (circumference formula) when asked for arc length.

Common Traps
  • Confusing arc length (a length) with sector area (a 2D region) — arc length uses 2πr; sector area uses πr².
  • Using the diameter as the radius in the formula — always halve the diameter before plugging into πr² or 2πr.
Key Takeaways
  • Circumference = 2πr. Area = πr². Both use r, not d.
  • Arc/sector = (θ/360°) × full-circle quantity. Think "fraction of the whole circle."
  • Inscribed angle = ½ × central angle for the same arc.
💡 Quick Tip

Memorise that a 60° central angle gives an arc that is 1/6 of the circumference, and a 90° angle gives 1/4. These fractions (1/6 and 1/4) come up repeatedly and are faster to recognise than computing from scratch.

Lesson 3

Quadrilaterals & Area

Objectives
  • Apply area formulas for rectangles, squares, parallelograms, trapezoids, and rhombuses.
  • Find the area of composite figures by breaking them into simpler shapes.
  • Distinguish perimeter (total boundary length) from area (enclosed region) and avoid mixing the two.
📌 Core Concept

All quadrilaterals have interior angles summing to 360°. Area formulas depend on the specific shape. The most important GMAT shapes are rectangles, squares, and triangles — parallelograms and trapezoids appear less frequently but their formulas are tested conceptually.

Key Formulas:
Rectangle: Area = l × w; Perimeter = 2(l + w)
Square: Area = s²; Perimeter = 4s; Diagonal = s√2
Parallelogram: Area = base × height (height ⊥ base)
Trapezoid: Area = ½ × (b₁ + b₂) × h
Rhombus: Area = ½ × d₁ × d₂ (d = diagonal)
How to Approach It
  1. 1Identify the shape and select the correct area formula.
  2. 2For parallelograms and trapezoids: confirm you are using the perpendicular height, not the slant side length.
  3. 3For composite figures: split into rectangles and triangles, compute each area separately, then add (or subtract for cut-outs).
  4. 4Always check whether the question asks for area (square units) or perimeter (linear units).
Example

A rectangle has length 12 and width 5. What is the length of its diagonal?

(A) 8.5(B) 10(C) 13(D) 14(E) 17
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). The diagonal of a rectangle is the hypotenuse of a right triangle with legs equal to the length and width. Recognise the 5-12-13 Pythagorean triple: diagonal = 13. Common trap: confusing the diagonal of a square (s√2) with the diagonal of a rectangle — the rectangle formula requires the Pythagorean theorem, not s√2.

Common Traps
  • Using the slant side as the height in a parallelogram — height must be perpendicular to the base. A slant side is always longer than the true perpendicular height.
  • Computing perimeter when the question asks for area (or vice versa) — re-read what is being asked before computing.
Key Takeaways
  • Rectangle diagonal: apply Pythagorean theorem to length and width.
  • Parallelogram area = base × perpendicular height. Side length ≠ height unless the shape is a rectangle.
  • Composite figures: decompose into triangles and rectangles; add or subtract as needed.
💡 Quick Tip

For composite figures, look for ways to create right angles — this lets you apply the Pythagorean theorem or a simple rectangle area formula, which is almost always faster than a general formula.

Lesson 4

Coordinate Geometry

Objectives
  • Calculate slope, midpoint, and distance between two points.
  • Write and interpret the equation of a line in slope-intercept form y = mx + b.
  • Identify parallel lines (equal slopes) and perpendicular lines (slopes that are negative reciprocals).
📌 Core Concept

Every point on the coordinate plane is (x, y). The slope of a line through (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂) is m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁). Slope measures rise over run — positive slope goes up-right, negative goes down-right, zero is horizontal, undefined is vertical.

Two lines are parallel if they have equal slopes. They are perpendicular if their slopes multiply to −1 (i.e., one slope is the negative reciprocal of the other).

Key Formulas:
Slope: m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁)
Midpoint: ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2)
Distance: √[(x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²]
Line: y = mx + b (m = slope, b = y-intercept)
Perpendicular slopes: m₁ × m₂ = −1
How to Approach It
  1. 1For slope: label your two points clearly, then compute (y₂ − y₁) ÷ (x₂ − x₁). Keep the sign consistent.
  2. 2For the equation of a line: find slope m first, then substitute one point to solve for b.
  3. 3For perpendicular lines: flip the slope and change the sign — e.g., if slope = 2/3, the perpendicular slope = −3/2.
  4. 4For distance: recognise common Pythagorean triples in disguise — the horizontal and vertical distances are the legs, the straight-line distance is the hypotenuse.
Example

Line ℓ passes through (1, 2) and (4, 8). What is the slope of a line perpendicular to ℓ?

(A) −2(B) −1/2(C) 1/2(D) 2(E) 3
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (B). Slope of ℓ = (8 − 2) / (4 − 1) = 6/3 = 2. Perpendicular slope = −1/2 (negative reciprocal of 2). Common trap: choosing (A) −2 by just negating the slope without taking the reciprocal — the perpendicular slope requires both flipping (reciprocal) and negating.

Common Traps
  • Negating the slope without taking the reciprocal for perpendicular lines — the perpendicular slope is the negative reciprocal: flip AND negate.
  • Mixing up Δy and Δx in the slope formula — slope = rise/run = (change in y) / (change in x), not the other way around.
Key Takeaways
  • Slope = Δy / Δx. Positive = upward; negative = downward; zero = horizontal; undefined = vertical.
  • Parallel lines: same slope. Perpendicular lines: slopes multiply to −1.
  • Distance formula is Pythagorean theorem applied to horizontal and vertical separations.
💡 Quick Tip

For perpendicular slopes: take your slope, flip it upside down (reciprocal), then change its sign. A slope of 3 becomes −1/3. A slope of −2/5 becomes +5/2. Two quick steps, always in that order.
