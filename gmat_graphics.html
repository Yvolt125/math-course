Lesson 1

Reading Charts (Bar, Line, Pie)

Objectives
  • Extract precise values from bar charts, line graphs, and pie charts.
  • Read axes carefully — identify units, scale, and whether axes start at zero.
  • Identify which data series each line or bar represents using the legend.
📌 Core Concept

Graphics Interpretation questions present a chart or graph and ask fill-in-the-blank or select-from-dropdown questions. Success depends on reading the graph accurately, especially when scales are non-standard or axes do not start at zero.

The most common errors come from misreading labels, misidentifying units, or confusing data series. A disciplined reading order — title, axes, legend, then data — prevents almost all of these errors.

How to Approach It
  1. 1Read the title and both axis labels (with units) before looking at the data.
  2. 2Note whether the y-axis starts at zero — a truncated axis makes differences look much larger than they are.
  3. 3For pie charts, confirm whether values are percentages or absolute numbers.
  4. 4Use the legend to identify each data series, then extract the specific value(s) the question asks about.
Example

The bar chart below shows quarterly revenue (in $M) for two products. The y-axis runs from 40 to 100. Legend: ■ Product A (dark), □ Product B (light).

QuarterProduct A ($M)Product B ($M)
Q15570
Q26080
Q37565
Q49050

In Q3, Product A's revenue was approximately what fraction of Product B's revenue?

(A) 7/13(B) 13/15(C) 15/13(D) 6/5(E) 5/4
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). Product A in Q3 = $75M; Product B in Q3 = $65M. Ratio = 75/65 = 15/13. Common trap: misreading the y-axis (which starts at 40, not 0) and estimating relative bar heights visually rather than reading the exact values.

Common Traps
  • Reading a bar chart with a non-zero baseline and overestimating the differences between bars — always check where the y-axis starts.
  • Confusing percentage of total with absolute value in pie charts — convert to actual numbers when the question asks for a count.
Summary
  • Always read axis labels and units before extracting values — title, axes, legend, then data.
  • Non-zero baselines distort apparent magnitude — check the y-axis origin every time.
  • Pie chart slices give proportions; convert to actual numbers using the given total when asked.
💡 Quick Tip

Spend 15 seconds reading the graph setup (title, axes, legend) before answering any question. This prevents misreading units or confusing two data series — the most common source of Graphics errors.

Lesson 2

Extracting Trends & Values

Objectives
  • Calculate percent change between two data points shown in a graph.
  • Identify whether a trend is increasing, decreasing, or flat over a given interval.
  • Estimate values between plotted points and compare trends across two data series.
📌 Core Concept

Beyond reading individual values, Graphics questions often require computation: percent change, ratios, or comparing trends across two data series. The common formula is percent change = (new − old) / old × 100%.

For trend questions, steeper slope means faster rate of change. When comparing two series, always anchor on the same x-axis value before comparing y-axis values.

How to Approach It
  1. 1Identify the two values to compare and read them carefully from the graph — write them on scratch paper.
  2. 2Apply the appropriate formula: percent change = (new − old) / old × 100%, or ratio, or difference.
  3. 3For trend questions, identify the direction (up/down) and rate of change (steep = fast).
  4. 4For cross-series comparisons, align on the same x-axis value before comparing y-axis values.
Example

Using the same quarterly revenue chart from Lesson 1 (Product A and Product B, Q1–Q4).

By approximately what percent did Product B's revenue change from Q2 to Q4?

(A) Decreased by approximately 25%(B) Decreased by approximately 37.5%(C) Decreased by approximately 50%(D) Increased by approximately 25%(E) Increased by approximately 60%
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (B). Q2 = $80M (old); Q4 = $50M (new). Percent change = (50 − 80) / 80 × 100% = −30/80 × 100% = −37.5%. A decrease of 37.5%. Common trap: computing (80 − 50) / 80 and getting +37.5% — the direction matters. New value goes in the numerator minus old value.

Common Traps
  • Using old − new instead of new − old in percent change calculations — this gives a negative result when the answer should be positive.
  • Comparing two data series at different x-axis points by mistake — always confirm you are reading the same x-value for both series.
Summary
  • Percent change = (new − old) / old × 100%. Order matters — new value goes in the numerator.
  • Steeper slope = faster rate of change. Flat segment = no change.
  • For cross-series comparisons, anchor on the same x-axis point before reading y-values.
💡 Quick Tip

Label extracted values on your scratch paper before computing. This prevents misremembering a value mid-calculation — a very common error when two readings are required.
