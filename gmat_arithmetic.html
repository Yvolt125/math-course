Lesson 1

Number Properties

Learning Objectives
  • Apply divisibility rules to quickly determine whether integers are divisible by 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 9, and 10.
  • Classify integers as prime, composite, odd, or even and predict how those classifications affect arithmetic operations.
  • Test boundary cases — zero, one, and negatives — that frequently appear in GMAT "must be true" questions.
📌 Core Concept

Number properties questions test divisibility rules, prime numbers, odd/even behavior, and integer properties. Understanding how numbers behave under arithmetic operations is essential. Mastering these rules lets you eliminate answer choices quickly without heavy computation.

How to Approach It
  1. 1Classify the number: is it positive or negative, odd or even, prime or composite?
  2. 2Apply divisibility rules — ÷2: number is even; ÷3: digit sum divisible by 3; ÷5: ends in 0 or 5.
  3. 3Test edge cases: 0, 1, and negative numbers — they behave differently from "ordinary" positives.
  4. 4For "must be true" questions, try to find a counterexample to eliminate wrong answers.
Example

If p is a prime number greater than 2, which of the following must be odd?

(A) p − 1(B) p + 1(C) p² + p(D) 2p + 1(E) p² − 1
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (D). Any prime > 2 is odd. So 2p is even, and 2p + 1 is always odd. Checking the others: (A) odd − 1 = even. (B) odd + 1 = even. (C) p(p+1) = odd × even = even. (E) (p−1)(p+1) = even × even = even.

Common Traps
  • Forgetting that 1 is neither prime nor composite, and 2 is the only even prime number — these two facts appear on the GMAT regularly.
  • In "must be true" questions with variables, only testing positive integers and missing negative or zero cases that would make the answer false.
Key Takeaways
  • Divisibility by 3: sum of digits divisible by 3. By 9: sum of digits divisible by 9.
  • The product of any even number of negatives is positive; an odd number of negatives gives a negative product.
  • Zero is even, neither positive nor negative, and divisible by every nonzero integer.
💡 Quick Tip

1 is not prime. 2 is the only even prime. Always test 0 and negative numbers in "must be true" questions — they are the most common sources of counterexamples.

Lesson 2

Fractions, Decimals & Percentages

Learning Objectives
  • Convert fluently between fraction, decimal, and percentage forms.
  • Apply benchmark fractions (1/4=0.25, 1/3≈0.333, 1/2=0.5, 2/3≈0.667, 3/4=0.75) to estimate and compare quickly.
  • Solve percent increase/decrease problems using the formula (change/original) × 100.
📌 Core Concept

Fractions, decimals, and percentages are three interchangeable representations of the same value. Fluency in converting between them is essential for speed on test day. Recognizing benchmark equivalents instantly reduces calculation time significantly.

How to Approach It
  1. 1Convert fractions to decimals by dividing the numerator by the denominator.
  2. 2Convert percentages to decimals by dividing by 100 (or move the decimal point left two places).
  3. 3Use benchmark fractions: 1/4 = 0.25, 1/3 ≈ 0.333, 1/2 = 0.5, 2/3 ≈ 0.667, 3/4 = 0.75.
  4. 4When comparing fractions with different denominators, cross-multiply to determine which is larger.
Example

A jacket originally costs $120. It is discounted by 25%, then the discounted price is increased by 10%. What is the final price?

(A) $90(B) $96(C) $99(D) $102(E) $108
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). Step 1: $120 × 0.75 = $90 (after 25% discount). Step 2: $90 × 1.10 = $99 (after 10% increase). Common trap: $120 × 0.85 = $102 (wrong — percent changes don't combine by subtraction).

Common Traps
  • Calculating percent change using the new value in the denominator instead of the original value — always divide by the starting amount.
  • Treating a percentage point change as a percentage change — "increased by 10 percentage points" and "increased by 10%" are different things.
Key Takeaways
  • To convert a fraction to a percent: divide numerator by denominator, then multiply by 100.
  • Percent increase = (new − old)/old × 100. Percent decrease = (old − new)/old × 100.
  • A 20% increase followed by a 20% decrease does NOT return to the original — it yields a 4% net decrease.
💡 Quick Tip

When comparing fractions, cross-multiply rather than finding a common denominator — it's faster. For a/b vs c/d, compare a×d with b×c directly.

Lesson 3

Ratios & Proportions

Learning Objectives
  • Set up and solve ratio problems using a multiplier k.
  • Distinguish part-to-part ratios from part-to-whole ratios and apply each correctly.
  • Solve proportion problems by cross-multiplying or scaling.
📌 Core Concept

A ratio compares two quantities; a proportion states that two ratios are equal. Ratios can be expressed as fractions, with a colon, or in words. Identifying whether a ratio is part-to-part or part-to-whole is the first critical step before solving.

How to Approach It
  1. 1Set up the ratio as a fraction and clarify whether it is part-to-part or part-to-whole.
  2. 2Assign a multiplier k: if a:b = 2:3, then a = 2k and b = 3k for some positive value k.
  3. 3Use any given total or sum to solve for k, then find the specific quantity the question asks for.
  4. 4Cross-multiply to solve proportions: if a/b = c/d, then ad = bc.
Example

In a class, the ratio of boys to girls is 3:5. If there are 40 students in total, how many more girls are there than boys?

(A) 5(B) 8(C) 10(D) 15(E) 25
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). Total parts = 3 + 5 = 8. Each part = 40 ÷ 8 = 5. Boys = 3 × 5 = 15, Girls = 5 × 5 = 25. Difference = 25 − 15 = 10.

Common Traps
  • Treating ratio values as actual quantities — if a:b = 3:5, the actual values are 3k and 5k for some k, not 3 and 5.
  • Solving for the ratio when the question asks for an actual quantity, or vice versa — read carefully which the question is requesting.
Key Takeaways
  • Assign a multiplier: if a:b = m:n, then a = mk and b = nk. Use a known total to solve for k.
  • Part-to-whole ratio = one part / sum of all parts.
  • In proportion problems, cross-multiply: a/b = c/d → ad = bc.
💡 Quick Tip

Always check whether the ratio is part-to-part or part-to-whole before solving. Confusing the two is the most common error on ratio questions.

Lesson 4

Exponents & Roots

Learning Objectives
  • Apply the five core exponent rules: product, quotient, power of a power, zero exponent, and negative exponent.
  • Simplify radical expressions by factoring out perfect squares.
  • Recognise how the relationship between the base and 1 affects the direction of scaling.
📌 Core Concept

Exponent rules govern multiplication, division, and powers of powers. Recognizing these patterns quickly is key to simplifying complex-looking expressions. Root rules follow naturally from exponent rules since √x = x^(1/2).

How to Approach It
  1. 1Apply the core rules: aᵐ × aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ; aᵐ / aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ; (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ.
  2. 2Remember special cases: a⁰ = 1 for any a ≠ 0; a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ.
  3. 3For roots: √(ab) = √a × √b. Simplify by factoring out perfect squares from under the radical.
  4. 4When bases differ, try to rewrite all terms with the same base before applying rules.
Example

If 2ˣ × 4² = 2¹⁰, what is the value of x?

(A) 4(B) 5(C) 6(D) 7(E) 8
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). Rewrite 4² = (2²)² = 2⁴. Then: 2ˣ × 2⁴ = 2¹⁰ → 2^(x+4) = 2¹⁰ → x + 4 = 10 → x = 6.

Common Traps
  • When 0 < base < 1, squaring makes the number smaller, not larger — the opposite of what happens with bases greater than 1.
  • Misapplying (a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b² by forgetting the middle term and writing a² + b² instead.
Key Takeaways
  • aᵐ × aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ; aᵐ / aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ; (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ; a⁰ = 1; a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ.
  • To simplify √72: factor out largest perfect square → √(36 × 2) = 6√2.
  • For bases between 0 and 1: larger exponent = smaller value. For bases > 1: larger exponent = larger value.
💡 Quick Tip

When the base is between 0 and 1, raising it to a higher power makes the number smaller. For example, (0.5)² = 0.25. This trips up many test-takers who assume bigger exponent = bigger result.

Lesson 5

Percent Change

Learning Objectives
  • Calculate percent increase and decrease using (new − old) / old × 100%.
  • Solve problems involving successive percent changes by multiplying multipliers (not adding the percentages).
  • Work backwards from a final value to find the original price using the inverse multiplier.
📌 Core Concept

Percent change = (new value − old value) / old value × 100%. The old value is always the denominator — it is the base from which the change is measured. A common error is dividing by the new value instead.

For successive percent changes, convert each change to a multiplier (e.g., +20% → ×1.2; −15% → ×0.85) and multiply all multipliers together. Do not add the percentages.

How to Approach It
  1. 1For a single percent change: % change = (new − old) / old × 100%. Positive = increase; negative = decrease.
  2. 2For successive changes: convert each to a multiplier and multiply them. A 20% increase then a 20% decrease = 1.2 × 0.8 = 0.96 (a net 4% decrease, not zero).
  3. 3To find the original price: divide the final price by the combined multiplier. E.g., if after a 25% markup the price is $75, original = $75 / 1.25 = $60.
  4. 4For markup/discount: New price = original × (1 + markup%) or × (1 − discount%).
Example

A jacket's price is increased by 25% and then decreased by 20%. What is the net percent change from the original price?

(A) −5%(B) 0%(C) +5%(D) −4%(E) +4%
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (A). Multiplier = 1.25 × 0.80 = 1.00. Wait — 1.25 × 0.80 = 1.00 exactly, so net change = 0%. Hmm, let me recalculate: 1.25 × 0.80 = 1.00. So answer is (B) 0%. Common trap for this variant: if the increases are different (e.g., +20% then −20%): 1.20 × 0.80 = 0.96 = a net −4% loss. Never add percentages for successive changes.

Common Traps
  • Adding successive percent changes — a +20% followed by a −20% does NOT equal 0%. You must multiply the multipliers: 1.2 × 0.8 = 0.96 = −4%.
  • Using the new value as the denominator — percent change always divides by the original (old) value, never the new one.
Key Takeaways
  • % change = (new − old) / old × 100%. Old value is always the base.
  • Successive changes: convert to multipliers and multiply. Never add percentages.
  • +p% increase → multiplier (1 + p/100); −p% decrease → multiplier (1 − p/100).
💡 Quick Tip

For "price up X% then down X%" questions, the net result is always a small loss — never zero. Use a real number to test: start at $100, go up 20% = $120, then down 20% = $96. The loss is X²/100 percent (here 4%).
