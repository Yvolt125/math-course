1. Matrices as Geometric Transformations Every matrix multiplication is a movement of space

In Week 1, a matrix was a container for data — a grid of numbers. In Week 2, we adopt the deeper view: a matrix is a machine that transforms space. When you compute \(A\mathbf{v}\), you are not just doing arithmetic — you are moving the point \(\mathbf{v}\) to a new location. Understanding this geometric view is the master key that unlocks eigenvalues, PCA, and why neural networks work.

The Core Insight — Multiplication as Movement

Given a matrix \(A\) and a vector \(\mathbf{v}\), the product \(A\mathbf{v}\) produces a new vector — the image of \(\mathbf{v}\) under the transformation \(A\). Apply \(A\) to every point in space simultaneously and you get a completely deformed space. The matrix defines the rule of deformation.

The four fundamental 2D transformations:

Scaling — stretch or compress along the axes: \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \] The x-component is tripled, the y-component is doubled. A unit square becomes a 3×2 rectangle.

Rotation by angle \(\theta\) — spin every vector around the origin: \[ R_\theta = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \] For \(\theta = 90°\): \(\begin{bmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix}\). The x-axis vector becomes the y-axis vector.

Shear — slant space like a tilted deck of cards: \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \begin{bmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x\\y\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}x+y\\y\end{bmatrix} \] Reflection — mirror space across an axis: \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{(flip across the x-axis)} \]

Column Space and Null Space — Where Vectors Go and Get Lost

Two fundamental subspaces tell you everything about what a matrix does:

  • Column Space (\(\text{Col}(A)\)): the set of all possible outputs — all vectors of the form \(A\mathbf{v}\) for every possible input \(\mathbf{v}\). This is where you can land. If \(A\) is an \(m \times n\) full-rank matrix, its column space fills all of \(\mathbb{R}^m\).
  • Null Space (\(\text{Null}(A)\)): the set of all inputs that get squashed to zero — all \(\mathbf{v}\) for which \(A\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\). A non-trivial null space (containing more than just \(\mathbf{0}\)) is the geometric signature of a singular matrix. The matrix destroys those directions entirely.
The Rank–Nullity Theorem: \[ \text{rank}(A) + \text{nullity}(A) = n \quad \text{(number of columns)} \] The rank (number of truly independent output directions) plus the nullity (number of input directions destroyed) always sums to the number of input dimensions. If a matrix squashes 1 dimension (nullity = 1), it shrinks the output by exactly 1 dimension (rank drops by 1). You cannot gain information; you can only lose it.
Neural Networks as a Chain of Transformations:

Each layer of a neural network applies a matrix multiplication \(W\mathbf{x}\) followed by a non-linear activation function. The network is literally a composition of geometric transformations — rotating, stretching, and bending the input space until, in the final layer, data points of different classes end up on opposite sides of a separating hyperplane. Understanding weight matrices as transformations explains why depth works: successive transformations can achieve complex separations that a single one cannot.

The Identity Matrix — The "Do Nothing" Transformation:

The Identity Matrix \(I\) leaves every vector exactly where it is: \(I\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}\). It is the transformation equivalent of multiplying by 1. From Week 1, remember that Gaussian Elimination drives the augmented matrix toward \([I \mid \text{solution}]\) — now you can see why: reaching \(I\) on the coefficient side means "the transformation has been completely undone."

Where we're headed: We now see matrices as movements of space. But to reason about how much two directions align — or how far a point is from a subspace — we need a way to measure the relationship between two vectors. That measure is the Dot Product.

2. The Dot Product How much do two vectors point in the same direction?

The Dot Product (also called the inner product) takes two vectors and produces a single number that quantifies their directional alignment. It is the most-used operation in all of Machine Learning — from the weights in a linear model to the attention scores in a transformer.

Two Equivalent Definitions

Algebraic (component-wise): \[ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + \cdots + a_n b_n = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i \]
Geometric (angle-based): \[ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \|\mathbf{a}\| \cdot \|\mathbf{b}\| \cdot \cos(\theta) \] where \(\theta\) is the angle between the two vectors and \(\|\mathbf{a}\| = \sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2 + \cdots + a_n^2}\) is the length (norm) of \(\mathbf{a}\).

These two formulas always agree. The algebraic one is fast to compute; the geometric one tells you what it means.

Reading the sign of the dot product:
  • \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} > 0\) → \(\cos\theta > 0\) → \(\theta < 90°\) — vectors point roughly the same way. They share a meaningful component in the same direction.
  • \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0\) → \(\cos\theta = 0\) → \(\theta = 90°\) — vectors are perpendicular (orthogonal). They share nothing — completely independent directions.
  • \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} < 0\) → \(\cos\theta < 0\) → \(\theta > 90°\) — vectors point in opposite directions.
Example: \(\mathbf{a} = [3, 4]\), \(\mathbf{b} = [1, 2]\) \[ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = (3)(1) + (4)(2) = 3 + 8 = 11 \] Since this is positive, the vectors point in broadly the same direction.

Orthogonality — The Most Important Special Case

Two vectors are Orthogonal if and only if their dot product is zero: \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0\). Orthogonal vectors carry completely independent information — knowing one tells you nothing about the other. This is the mathematical ideal for features in a machine learning model.

The dot product is everywhere in ML:
  • Linear model prediction: \(\hat{y} = \mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x} + b\). The prediction is literally a dot product between the weight vector and the feature vector — measuring how much the input aligns with what the model has learned to look for.
  • Cosine similarity (NLP, recommender systems): \(\cos(\theta) = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\|\mathbf{a}\|\|\mathbf{b}\|}\). Normalizing the dot product gives a pure alignment score between −1 and +1, regardless of vector length. Word vectors with high cosine similarity have similar meanings.
  • Attention mechanism (transformers): \(\text{score}(Q, K) = \frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\). The attention score between a query and a key is a scaled dot product — it measures how relevant each key is to the query, in the geometric sense of alignment.
Where we're headed: The dot product tells us how much two vectors align. A natural next question: if a vector is not perfectly aligned with a subspace, what is the closest point in that subspace to where it is? Finding that closest point is called a Projection — and it is the geometric heart of linear regression.

3. Projections and Least Squares The closest approximation when no exact answer exists

In the real world, a system \(A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\) is usually overdetermined — more equations than unknowns, more data points than parameters. No exact solution exists because \(\mathbf{b}\) does not lie in the column space of \(A\). A Projection finds the closest point that does lie in the column space — the best possible approximation. This is the mathematical foundation of linear regression.

Projecting a Vector onto a Direction

The projection of vector \(\mathbf{a}\) onto the direction of vector \(\mathbf{b}\) is the shadow of \(\mathbf{a}\) cast straight down onto the line through \(\mathbf{b}\):

\[ \text{proj}_{\mathbf{b}}(\mathbf{a}) = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{b}} \, \mathbf{b} = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\|\mathbf{b}\|^2} \, \mathbf{b} \]

The scalar \(\frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\|\mathbf{b}\|^2}\) tells you "how many times \(\mathbf{b}\)" you need, and multiplying by \(\mathbf{b}\) itself gives you the actual projected vector. The key property: the error \(\mathbf{a} - \text{proj}_{\mathbf{b}}(\mathbf{a})\) is always perpendicular to \(\mathbf{b}\). Perpendicularity is what "closest" means in this geometry.

Concrete example: \(\mathbf{a} = [3, 4]\), \(\mathbf{b} = [1, 0]\) (the x-axis) \[ \text{proj} = \frac{[3,4] \cdot [1,0]}{[1,0] \cdot [1,0]} [1,0] = \frac{3}{1}[1,0] = [3, 0] \] The projection is \([3, 0]\) — the foot of the perpendicular from \([3,4]\) to the x-axis. The error vector \([3,4] - [3,0] = [0,4]\) is indeed perpendicular to \([1,0]\) (their dot product is 0). ✓

Projecting onto a Subspace — The Normal Equations

When \(A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\) has no exact solution (because \(\mathbf{b}\) is not in the column space of \(A\)), we instead find the \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) that makes \(A\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) as close to \(\mathbf{b}\) as possible. The condition for this is that the error \(\mathbf{b} - A\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) must be perpendicular to every column of \(A\) — i.e., \(A^T(\mathbf{b} - A\hat{\mathbf{x}}) = \mathbf{0}\), which gives:

\[ A^T A \hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T \mathbf{b} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \hat{\mathbf{x}} = (A^T A)^{-1} A^T \mathbf{b} \]
This IS the linear regression formula — now you know where it comes from:

In linear regression, \(A\) is your design matrix \(X\) (each row is a data point), \(\mathbf{b}\) is the vector of observed outputs \(\mathbf{y}\), and \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) is the weight vector \(\mathbf{w}\). Because in real data no straight line passes through every point, there is no exact solution — so we project \(\mathbf{y}\) onto the column space of \(X\) and pick the closest fit:

\[ \mathbf{w} = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T \mathbf{y} \]

This is not a mysterious formula — it is the algebraic result of the geometric condition "the prediction vector \(X\mathbf{w}\) should be the projection of \(\mathbf{y}\) onto the column space of \(X\)." The residual \(\mathbf{y} - X\mathbf{w}\) will be perpendicular to every predictor in the model. This perpendicularity is exactly why ordinary least squares minimizes the sum of squared errors.

Where we're headed: Projections tell us how to find the closest vector in a subspace. But certain special vectors react to matrix transformations in a uniquely simple way — they don't get rotated at all, only scaled. These special vectors are the Eigenvectors, and their scaling factors are the Eigenvalues. They are the single most important concept in applied linear algebra.

4. Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors The invariant directions — axes that a matrix can only stretch, not rotate

Most vectors change direction when multiplied by a matrix. But every matrix has a handful of special vectors that it can only scale — never rotate. These are the Eigenvectors. The amount by which each is scaled is the corresponding Eigenvalue. Together they reveal the natural structure hidden inside any matrix.

The Defining Equation

\[ A\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v} \]

Read this as: "Multiplying the eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}\) by matrix \(A\) produces the same vector back, just scaled by \(\lambda\)." The direction of \(\mathbf{v}\) is unchanged by \(A\) — it is an invariant axis of the transformation. \(\lambda\) (lambda) can be any real number:

  • \(\lambda > 1\): the eigenvector is stretched longer in its direction.
  • \(0 < \lambda < 1\): the eigenvector is compressed shorter.
  • \(\lambda < 0\): the eigenvector is flipped (reversed direction) and possibly scaled.
  • \(\lambda = 0\): the eigenvector is squashed to zero — this means the matrix is singular (det = 0). A zero eigenvalue is the algebraic fingerprint of singularity.

Finding Eigenvalues — The Characteristic Equation

Rearrange \(A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}\) to get \((A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\). For a non-zero eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}\) to exist, the matrix \((A - \lambda I)\) must be singular — it must crush space so that \(\mathbf{v}\) lands on zero. From Week 1 we know: singular ↔ determinant = 0. So:

\[ \det(A - \lambda I) = 0 \quad \longleftarrow \text{The Characteristic Equation} \]

Solving this polynomial in \(\lambda\) gives all eigenvalues. An \(n \times n\) matrix has exactly \(n\) eigenvalues (counting multiplicity, possibly complex).

Worked Example: Find the eigenvalues of \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\)

1 Set up the characteristic equation: \[ \det(A - \lambda I) = \det\begin{bmatrix} 3-\lambda & 1 \\ 0 & 2-\lambda \end{bmatrix} = (3-\lambda)(2-\lambda) - (1)(0) = 0 \] 2 Expand and solve: \[ \lambda^2 - 5\lambda + 6 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad (\lambda - 3)(\lambda - 2) = 0 \] \[ \boxed{\lambda_1 = 3, \quad \lambda_2 = 2} \] 3 Find eigenvectors for each eigenvalue:
For \(\lambda_1 = 3\): solve \((A - 3I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\) \[ \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \] For \(\lambda_2 = 2\): solve \((A - 2I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\) \[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \] Verification: \(A\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}3&1\\0&2\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}3\\0\end{bmatrix} = 3 \cdot \begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix}\) ✓   The matrix stretches \(\mathbf{v}_1\) by 3 in its own direction without any rotation.

Geometric Interpretation — The Transformation Visualized

The interactive graph below shows the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}\) applied to every point on the unit circle. The circle stretches into an ellipse. The two eigenvectors — \(\mathbf{v}_1 = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}[1,1]\) (stretched by \(\lambda_1 = 3\)) and \(\mathbf{v}_2 = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}[1,-1]\) (unchanged, \(\lambda_2 = 1\)) — are the only two directions that the transformation does not rotate. Everything else gets swept into a new direction; only the eigenvectors stay put.

Key Properties of Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors:
  • Trace = sum of eigenvalues: \(\text{tr}(A) = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \cdots + \lambda_n\)
  • Determinant = product of eigenvalues: \(\det(A) = \lambda_1 \cdot \lambda_2 \cdots \lambda_n\)
  • Therefore: \(\det(A) = 0 \Leftrightarrow\) at least one eigenvalue is zero — singular matrix (Week 1 connection).
  • Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are always linearly independent.
  • Eigenvectors are not unique — any scalar multiple of an eigenvector is also an eigenvector.
Where we're headed: We now know how to find eigenvalues and eigenvectors for any matrix. For a special class of matrices — symmetric ones, which include every covariance matrix in data science — the eigenvectors have an additional magical property: they are always orthogonal. This, combined with Diagonalization, gives us the most useful matrix decomposition in all of ML.

5. Symmetric Matrices and Diagonalization The cleanest matrices — and why they power all of data science

A Symmetric Matrix is one where \(A = A^T\) — it is identical to its own transpose. Every entry above the diagonal mirrors the entry below it. These matrices are not a special curiosity — they are the most common matrices in ML. Any matrix of the form \(X^T X\) is symmetric. Every covariance matrix is symmetric. The kernel (Gram) matrices in SVMs are symmetric. And for symmetric matrices, eigendecomposition is not just possible — it is perfect.

The Spectral Theorem — The Guarantee for Symmetric Matrices

The Spectral Theorem: Every real symmetric matrix \(S\) satisfies:
  1. All eigenvalues of \(S\) are real numbers (never complex).
  2. Eigenvectors corresponding to different eigenvalues are orthogonal — they point in completely independent directions.
  3. \(S\) can always be fully diagonalized. No exceptions.
This is not true for general matrices. For symmetric matrices, it is always guaranteed.

Diagonalization — Decomposing a Matrix into Its Essence

A matrix \(A\) is Diagonalizable if it can be written as:

\[ A = P D P^{-1} \]

where \(D\) is a diagonal matrix containing the eigenvalues, and \(P\) is the matrix whose columns are the corresponding eigenvectors. For a symmetric matrix \(S\), this simplifies beautifully because its eigenvectors are orthogonal — so \(P^{-1} = P^T\):

\[ S = Q D Q^T \quad \text{(the Eigendecomposition / Spectral Decomposition)} \]

where \(Q\) is an orthogonal matrix (columns are unit-length orthogonal eigenvectors), and \(D\) is diagonal (eigenvalues on the diagonal). This decomposition says: the symmetric matrix \(S\) is nothing more than "rotate to the eigenvector basis, scale each axis by the corresponding eigenvalue, then rotate back."

Why diagonalization is so powerful:
  • Matrix powers become trivial: \(A^n = PD^nP^{-1}\), and \(D^n\) is just raising each diagonal entry to the \(n\)th power. Computing \(A^{1000}\) is suddenly just as easy as computing \(A\).
  • Solving differential equations: Systems like \(\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A\mathbf{x}\) become decoupled in the eigenvector basis — each component evolves independently by \(e^{\lambda_i t}\).
  • Understanding the transformation completely: In the eigenvector basis, the matrix is purely diagonal — it stretches each axis independently. There is no mixing. The diagonal form is the "simplest possible view" of the matrix.

Positive Definite and Positive Semi-Definite Matrices

For covariance matrices and kernel matrices, we need one more property. A symmetric matrix \(S\) is:

NameConditionEigenvaluesML Significance
Positive Definite (PD) \(\mathbf{v}^T S \mathbf{v} > 0\) for all \(\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}\) All \(\lambda > 0\) Full rank; invertible; loss function bowls upward (guarantees a unique minimum)
Positive Semi-Definite (PSD) \(\mathbf{v}^T S \mathbf{v} \geq 0\) for all \(\mathbf{v}\) All \(\lambda \geq 0\) May be singular; covariance matrices are always PSD (variance is never negative)

A covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) is always symmetric and positive semi-definite. Its eigenvalues are always non-negative — and each one measures how much variance the data has in the corresponding eigenvector direction.

Where we're headed: Diagonalization is most powerful when applied to the matrix that summarizes all variability in a dataset: the Covariance Matrix. Understanding the covariance matrix is the last stepping stone before PCA.

6. The Covariance Matrix The geometric fingerprint of your dataset's variability

The Covariance Matrix \(\Sigma\) is a single symmetric matrix that encodes, for every pair of features in your dataset, how much they vary together. It is the most complete statistical summary of a dataset's geometric shape — and it is the direct input to PCA.

Building Up from Variance to Covariance

  • Variance of a single feature \(x\): \(\text{Var}(x) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2\) — the average squared distance from the mean. High variance = spread out data; low variance = clustered data.
  • Covariance between two features \(x\) and \(y\): \(\text{Cov}(x, y) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})\)
    • Positive: when one feature is above its mean, the other tends to be too (correlated).
    • Zero: the features are uncorrelated (orthogonal directions of variation).
    • Negative: when one is above its mean, the other tends to be below (anti-correlated).

The Full Covariance Matrix

For a dataset \(X\) (already centered — mean subtracted from each feature column), the covariance matrix is:

\[ \Sigma = \frac{1}{n} X^T X \]

For a dataset with \(p\) features, \(\Sigma\) is a \(p \times p\) symmetric matrix. Its \((i,j)\) entry is the covariance between feature \(i\) and feature \(j\). The diagonal entries are the variances of individual features.

3-feature dataset example: \[ \Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \text{Var}(x_1) & \text{Cov}(x_1,x_2) & \text{Cov}(x_1,x_3) \\ \text{Cov}(x_1,x_2) & \text{Var}(x_2) & \text{Cov}(x_2,x_3) \\ \text{Cov}(x_1,x_3) & \text{Cov}(x_2,x_3) & \text{Var}(x_3) \end{bmatrix} \] Notice it is symmetric — \(\text{Cov}(x_1, x_2) = \text{Cov}(x_2, x_1)\). The diagonal captures individual spread; the off-diagonal entries capture pairwise relationships.

If \(\Sigma\) is diagonal (all off-diagonal entries are zero), the features are uncorrelated — they vary in entirely independent directions. This is the "clean" case.
If \(\Sigma\) has large off-diagonal entries, features are highly correlated — they carry redundant information. A feature's column space overlaps with another's. Rank drops. The matrix becomes nearly singular. This is the "messy" case — and it's exactly what PCA fixes.
The Geometric Picture:

The covariance matrix defines an ellipsoid in feature space — a stretched, rotated egg shape that shows where the data lives. The eigenvectors of \(\Sigma\) point along the principal axes of this ellipsoid (the directions of maximum spread). The eigenvalues give the length of each axis — larger eigenvalue = more data spread in that direction. PCA's job is to find and use these axes.

Why correlated features are dangerous:

From Week 1: the linear regression formula requires computing \((X^TX)^{-1}\). If features are highly correlated, \(X^TX\) (= \(n \cdot \Sigma\)) becomes nearly singular (near-zero determinant, rank-deficient). Inverting a near-singular matrix amplifies floating-point errors explosively — small measurement noise causes wildly unstable weight estimates. This is called multicollinearity. Ridge regression adds a small term \(\lambda I\) to \(X^TX\) before inverting, ensuring positive definiteness and stability.

Where we're headed: The covariance matrix's eigenvectors point in the directions of maximum variance. Projecting data onto these directions — keeping only the most informative ones — is Principal Component Analysis. PCA is the direct application of every concept in this guide.

7. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) Finding the natural axes of your data — and throwing away the rest

PCA is a dimensionality reduction technique that finds the directions of maximum variance in a dataset and projects the data onto a lower-dimensional space aligned with those directions. It is the direct application of eigendecomposition to the covariance matrix — and the culmination of everything in Week 2.

The Problem PCA Solves

In ML, datasets often have dozens, hundreds, or thousands of features. Many of these features are correlated — they carry overlapping information. The "true" dimensionality of the data — the number of genuinely independent directions of variation — is often much lower than the number of raw features. PCA finds this true dimensionality and discards the redundant dimensions, reducing noise and computational cost while retaining the essential structure.

The PCA Algorithm — Step by Step

1 Center the data.
Subtract the mean of each feature column from every observation: \[ \tilde{X} = X - \bar{X} \] Without centering, the first principal component would point toward the data's centroid rather than the direction of maximum spread.

2 Compute the covariance matrix. \[ \Sigma = \frac{1}{n} \tilde{X}^T \tilde{X} \] This \(p \times p\) symmetric matrix encodes all pairwise variances and covariances among the \(p\) features.

3 Find the eigenvectors and eigenvalues of \(\Sigma\). \[ \Sigma \mathbf{q}_i = \lambda_i \mathbf{q}_i \] The eigenvectors \(\mathbf{q}_1, \mathbf{q}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{q}_p\) are orthogonal (from the Spectral Theorem). The eigenvalues \(\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_p \geq 0\) measure the variance in each eigenvector direction.

4 Sort by eigenvalue (descending).
The eigenvector with the largest eigenvalue is the First Principal Component (PC1) — the direction of maximum variance. PC2 is the next (orthogonal) direction of maximum remaining variance. And so on.

5 Choose how many components to keep.
Decide on \(k < p\) based on how much variance you want to retain (see explained variance ratio below). Form the projection matrix \(Q_k = [\mathbf{q}_1 | \mathbf{q}_2 | \cdots | \mathbf{q}_k]\), a \(p \times k\) matrix.

6 Project the data. \[ Z = \tilde{X} Q_k \] \(Z\) is your reduced dataset: \(n\) observations × \(k\) principal components. The data is now in the natural coordinate system of the data's own variability.

Explained Variance Ratio — How Much Information Did You Keep?

\[ \text{Explained Variance Ratio for PC}_i = \frac{\lambda_i}{\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \cdots + \lambda_p} \]

The fraction of total variance explained by the \(i\)th principal component. Sum the top \(k\) ratios to find the total variance retained by \(k\) components. A common rule of thumb: keep enough components to explain ≥ 95% of total variance.

Reading an explained variance plot: Suppose a 10-feature dataset has eigenvalues \([6.2, 2.1, 0.8, 0.4, 0.3, \ldots]\) summing to 10.
  • PC1 explains 62% of variance (λ₁/total = 6.2/10)
  • PC2 explains 21% (2.1/10)
  • Together: 83%. Keeping 3 components gets to 91%.
You could reduce from 10 dimensions to 3 while retaining 91% of all information.

PCA Visualized — Principal Components on Real Data

The interactive graph below shows a 2D dataset with a clear correlation between the two features. PC1 (the first principal component, in green) runs along the direction of maximum variance — the "spine" of the data cloud. PC2 (in orange) is perpendicular — it captures the remaining, smaller variation. Projecting onto PC1 alone preserves most of the information while reducing from 2D to 1D.

PCA in Practice — Where You'll Encounter It:
  • Dimensionality reduction before ML: Reduce 500 features to 20 principal components before feeding to a classifier. Faster training, less overfitting.
  • Visualization: Project high-dimensional data (word embeddings, images) down to 2D or 3D for human inspection using the top 2 or 3 PCs.
  • Noise reduction: Small eigenvalues correspond to directions with little variance — often just noise. Discarding those components effectively denoises the data.
  • Face recognition (Eigenfaces): Each image is a vector of pixels. PCA finds "eigenfaces" — the directions of maximum variation across all faces. Any face can be reconstructed as a linear combination of eigenfaces.
  • Removing multicollinearity: Project correlated features onto uncorrelated principal components before linear regression, eliminating near-singularity in \(X^TX\).
PCA and SVD — The Same Thing, Computed Differently:

In practice, PCA is almost never computed via the covariance matrix's eigenvectors. Instead, we use the Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the centered data matrix: \(\tilde{X} = U \Sigma V^T\). The columns of \(V\) are exactly the principal component directions; the singular values in \(\Sigma\) are the square roots of the covariance matrix's eigenvalues. SVD is numerically more stable — it avoids forming \(\tilde{X}^T\tilde{X}\) explicitly, which can amplify rounding errors. When you call sklearn.decomposition.PCA, it uses SVD internally.

Where we're headed: The theory is complete. Every concept — transformations, dot products, projections, eigenvalues, eigenvectors, diagonalization, covariance, PCA — now lives in Python with three lines of code.

8. Python with NumPy and scikit-learn Implementing every Week 2 concept at production scale

The key NumPy functions for Week 2:
  • np.dot(a, b) or a @ b — dot product (vectors) or matrix multiplication (matrices)
  • np.linalg.norm(v) — vector length \(\|\mathbf{v}\|\)
  • np.linalg.eig(A) — eigenvalues and eigenvectors of any square matrix (may return complex numbers)
  • np.linalg.eigh(S) — eigenvalues and eigenvectors of a symmetric matrix (always real, sorted, stable — prefer this for covariance matrices)
  • np.linalg.svd(X) — Singular Value Decomposition of any matrix
  • np.cov(X.T) — covariance matrix (NumPy divides by \(n-1\) by default)
  • sklearn.decomposition.PCA — full PCA pipeline using SVD internally

# Cosine similarity between two vectors
cos_sim = np.dot(a, b) / (np.linalg.norm(a) * np.linalg.norm(b))

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# § 2 — PROJECTION
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
a = np.array([3, 4])
b = np.array([1, 0])        # project a onto b (x-axis)
proj = (np.dot(a, b) / np.dot(b, b)) * b   # [3, 0]

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# § 3 — EIGENVALUES AND EIGENVECTORS
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
S = np.array([[4.0, 2.0],
              [2.0, 3.0]])  # Symmetric matrix

# For symmetric matrices, ALWAYS use eigh — guaranteed real,
# returns eigenvalues in ascending order, more numerically stable
eigenvalues, eigenvectors = np.linalg.eigh(S)
# eigenvalues: [1.76, 5.24]  (ascending)
# eigenvectors: columns are the eigenvectors

# Verify: S @ v = λ * v
idx = np.argmax(eigenvalues)          # index of largest eigenvalue
lam = eigenvalues[idx]
evec = eigenvectors[:, idx]
print(np.allclose(S @ evec, lam * evec))   # True

# Sort descending (eigendecomposition usually wants largest first)
order = np.argsort(eigenvalues)[::-1]
eigenvalues  = eigenvalues[order]
eigenvectors = eigenvectors[:, order]

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# § 4 — PCA FROM SCRATCH
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# Simulated correlated dataset: 100 observations × 3 features
np.random.seed(42)
n = 100
t = np.random.randn(n)
X = np.column_stack([
    t + 0.3 * np.random.randn(n),   # Feature 1 ≈ t
    0.8 * t + 0.3 * np.random.randn(n),  # Feature 2 ≈ 0.8t  (correlated)
    np.random.randn(n)               # Feature 3: pure noise
])

# Step 1: center the data
X_c = X - X.mean(axis=0)

# Step 2: covariance matrix (divide by n, not n-1, for consistency)
cov = X_c.T @ X_c / n               # 3 × 3 symmetric PSD matrix

# Step 3: eigendecomposition of covariance matrix
vals, vecs = np.linalg.eigh(cov)

# Step 4: sort descending
order = np.argsort(vals)[::-1]
vals  = vals[order]
vecs  = vecs[:, order]

# Step 5: explained variance ratio
total   = vals.sum()
ratios  = vals / total
cumulative = np.cumsum(ratios)
print(f"PC1 explains {ratios[0]*100:.1f}% of variance")
print(f"PC1+PC2 explains {cumulative[1]*100:.1f}% of variance")

# Step 6: project to 2D (keep top 2 components)
Q2   = vecs[:, :2]           # 3 × 2 projection matrix
Z    = X_c @ Q2              # 100 × 2 reduced dataset

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# § 5 — PCA USING SCIKIT-LEARN (RECOMMENDED FOR PRODUCTION)
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
pca = PCA(n_components=2)
Z_sk = pca.fit_transform(X)

print(pca.explained_variance_ratio_)   # fraction of variance per component
print(pca.components_)                 # principal component vectors (rows)

# To choose n_components automatically to explain ≥ 95% variance:
pca_auto = PCA(n_components=0.95)      # keeps minimum components for 95%
pca_auto.fit(X)

The Chain of Understanding — Week 2 in Flow

Each concept answers a question raised by the one before it, culminating in one of the most important algorithms in all of Machine Learning.

1
Matrices as Geometric Transformations
"What does a matrix actually do?"
A matrix transforms space — rotating, scaling, shearing, or reflecting every vector simultaneously. Column space is where vectors land; null space is what gets destroyed. Neural network layers are a chain of these transformations.
Space can be transformed. But how do we measure the relationship between two directions in space?
2
The Dot Product
"How much do two vectors align?"
\(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \|\mathbf{a}\|\|\mathbf{b}\|\cos\theta\). Positive = same direction; zero = perpendicular (orthogonal, independent); negative = opposite. The foundational operation of cosine similarity, linear models, and attention mechanisms.
We can measure alignment. When no exact solution exists, what is the best approximate answer?
3
Projections and Least Squares
"What is the closest point in a subspace to a given vector?"
The projection minimizes distance by making the error perpendicular to the subspace. This produces the Normal Equations \((A^TA)\hat{x} = A^Tb\) — the geometric derivation of the linear regression formula \(w = (X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty\).
We can project onto subspaces. Are there vectors that a transformation never rotates — only scales?
4
Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors
"Which directions does this matrix leave unchanged?"
\(A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}\). Eigenvectors are the invariant axes of a transformation — they only scale (by \(\lambda\)), never rotate. Found via the characteristic equation \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\). A zero eigenvalue means singularity.
Eigenvectors exist for any matrix. For symmetric matrices, they have an additional critical property.
5
Symmetric Matrices and Diagonalization
"What is special about symmetric matrices, and how do we decompose them?"
The Spectral Theorem: symmetric matrices always have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors. This gives the clean decomposition \(S = QDQ^T\) — rotate to eigenvector basis, scale, rotate back. Positive definite = all \(\lambda > 0\) = bowl-shaped loss surface.
Diagonalization works best on symmetric matrices. Which symmetric matrix summarizes our entire dataset?
6
The Covariance Matrix
"How do we capture all pairwise variability in a dataset?"
\(\Sigma = \frac{1}{n}X^TX\) (after centering). Symmetric, PSD. Diagonal entries = variances; off-diagonal = covariances. Large off-diagonal entries signal correlated features, near-singularity, and unstable regression. The covariance matrix defines the ellipsoidal shape of the data cloud.
The covariance matrix has eigenvectors. Those eigenvectors point where the data varies most. Now use them.
7
PCA — Principal Component Analysis
"How do we find the true, low-dimensional structure of high-dimensional data?"
Center → covariance matrix → eigendecomposition → sort by eigenvalue → project onto top \(k\) eigenvectors. PC1 is the direction of maximum variance; PC2 the next orthogonal direction; and so on. Eigenvalue = variance explained. Keep components summing to ≥ 95% variance.
Theory complete. Three lines of NumPy or scikit-learn brings all of this to life.
8
Python with NumPy and scikit-learn
"How do we apply all of Week 2 in production code?"
np.dot / @ (dot product & matrix multiply) · np.linalg.eigh (symmetric eigendecomposition) · np.linalg.svd (decompose any matrix) · PCA(n_components=k).fit_transform(X) (full pipeline). Every concept, one library.
