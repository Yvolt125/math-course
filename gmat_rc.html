Lesson 1

Main Idea

🎯 LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Identify the central argument or thesis of a GMAT reading passage
  • Distinguish main idea answers from those that are too narrow (detail) or too broad (beyond the passage)
  • Predict what a correct main idea answer looks like before reading choices
📌 CORE CONCEPT

Identifying the central argument or purpose of the passage is the foundation of RC. The main idea ties all paragraphs together and reflects the author's primary reason for writing. Every other piece of content in the passage supports, illustrates, or qualifies this central point.

How to Approach It
  • 1Read the first and last sentences of each paragraph to capture the structural skeleton of the passage.
  • 2Ask yourself: "What is the author's main point?" — state it in one sentence before looking at answer choices.
  • 3Eliminate choices that are too narrow (focus on a single detail) or too broad (go beyond what the passage covers).
Example Question

Before standardized timekeeping, each British town kept local solar time, making railway schedules chaotic — a train departing "at noon" meant something different in Bristol than in London. Railway companies imposed a single standard time in 1847. Critics viewed this as an artificial imposition on natural rhythms, yet within a generation standard time had become simply "time," its railway origins largely forgotten.

The passage is primarily concerned with:

(A) The scientific reasons why solar time varies by geographic location (B) How railway companies introduced and popularised standardized time (C) The social opposition that halted time standardization efforts (D) Differences between British and American railway scheduling practices (E) The long-term benefits of standard time for modern transportation
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (B). The passage traces how railway companies introduced standard time and how it eventually became invisible — covering both origin and acceptance. (A) is out of scope. (C) overstates; resistance was overcome. (D) is a detail, not the main point. (E) is not discussed.

⚠️ COMMON TRAPS
  • Choosing an answer that accurately describes one paragraph but ignores the rest of the passage — this is a "too narrow" trap
  • Choosing an answer that correctly describes the topic but misrepresents the author's stance or scope
📋 LESSON SUMMARY
  • The main idea spans the entire passage, not just one part
  • Correct answers often use words like "argues," "explains," or "describes" — match the author's purpose
  • Eliminate answers that are factually accurate but too specific or too general
💡 QUICK TIP

Main idea answers never focus on a single detail — they cover the whole passage. If an answer choice accurately describes only one paragraph, eliminate it.

Lesson 2

Detail & Inference

🎯 LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Locate specific information stated in the passage to answer detail questions
  • Identify what must logically follow from stated facts for inference questions
  • Distinguish between what is stated, what is implied, and what is outside the scope of the passage
📌 CORE CONCEPT

Detail questions ask what the passage explicitly states — find the exact line and match it. Inference questions ask what must logically follow from the stated information. Both types require you to stay tightly anchored to the text rather than relying on outside knowledge.

How to Approach It
  • 1Return to the relevant paragraph identified in the question stem before reading the answer choices.
  • 2Find the exact lines referenced — underline or mentally flag the key phrase that answers the question.
  • 3Eliminate answers that go beyond what's stated, introduce new assumptions, or contradict the passage.
Example Question

Before standardized timekeeping, each British town kept local solar time, making railway schedules chaotic — a train departing "at noon" meant something different in Bristol than in London. Railway companies imposed a single standard time in 1847. Critics viewed this as an artificial imposition on natural rhythms, yet within a generation standard time had become simply "time," its railway origins largely forgotten.

According to the passage, railway companies adopted standard time primarily because:

(A) Government regulations required a uniform national timekeeping standard (B) Travelers complained about clock inconsistencies when crossing town boundaries (C) Different towns' local times made it impossible to coordinate train schedules (D) The scientific community had already established international time conventions (E) Competition among railway companies created pressure to standardize
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). The passage explicitly states schedules were "chaotic" because noon meant different things in different towns. (A), (B), (D), and (E) are not mentioned in the passage.

⚠️ COMMON TRAPS
  • Selecting an answer that sounds reasonable but requires knowledge or assumptions not supported by the passage
  • Confusing detail questions (what does the passage say?) with inference questions (what must follow from what the passage says?)
📋 LESSON SUMMARY
  • For detail questions: find the relevant lines, then choose the answer closest to what those lines say
  • For inference questions: the correct answer must be true given the passage — not merely possible
  • "Must be true" is a higher standard than "could be true"
💡 QUICK TIP

Inference answers are strongly supported by the text — never a big leap. If you find yourself filling in information the passage never hinted at, the answer is almost certainly wrong.

Lesson 3

Author's Tone & Purpose

🎯 LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Identify the author's attitude toward the subject (positive, negative, neutral, critical, analytical)
  • Determine why specific rhetorical choices are made (to illustrate, to qualify, to contrast)
  • Select the answer that correctly captures both tone and purpose
📌 CORE CONCEPT

Tone reflects the author's attitude toward the subject — whether they are critical, supportive, neutral, or cautious. Purpose reflects why they wrote the passage — to argue, inform, describe, or evaluate. Distinguishing between the two helps you answer both tone-specific and purpose-specific questions accurately.

How to Approach It
  • 1Note emotionally charged or evaluative words throughout the passage — they signal the author's stance.
  • 2Identify whether the author argues a position, describes a phenomenon, or evaluates competing views.
  • 3Match the tone precisely to answer choices — avoid choices that are too positive or too negative relative to the passage.
Example Question

Before standardized timekeeping, each British town kept local solar time, making railway schedules chaotic — a train departing "at noon" meant something different in Bristol than in London. Railway companies imposed a single standard time in 1847. Critics viewed this as an artificial imposition on natural rhythms, yet within a generation standard time had become simply "time," its railway origins largely forgotten.

The author's attitude toward those who resisted standard time is best described as:

(A) Sympathetic — the author presents their concerns as fundamentally valid (B) Dismissive — the author implies their resistance was irrational (C) Neutral — the author reports their position without endorsing or criticizing it (D) Admiring — the passage suggests their stand for solar tradition was praiseworthy (E) Critical — the author argues the resistance caused unnecessary delays
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). The author uses the neutral phrase "critics viewed this as an artificial imposition" — no judgment is offered on whether the critics were right or wrong. The tone is purely descriptive.

⚠️ COMMON TRAPS
  • Choosing a strongly positive or negative tone when GMAT academic passages almost always use measured, analytical language
  • Confusing the tone of a source the author cites with the author's own tone
📋 LESSON SUMMARY
  • GMAT passages rarely express strong emotion — expect "critical but respectful" or "analytical" tones
  • Purpose answers describe what the author does, not what the passage is about
  • Watch for words like "surprisingly," "however," and "despite" — they signal the author's perspective
💡 QUICK TIP

GMAT passages rarely express strong emotion — look for "measured," "analytical," or "critical" tones rather than "outraged" or "enthusiastic." When in doubt, lean toward the more restrained description.

Lesson 4

Function of a Passage / Paragraph

🎯 LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Identify the structural role of a specific sentence, example, or paragraph within the passage
  • Understand how individual parts support, contrast with, or qualify the main argument
  • Describe function in terms of what the element does, not what it says
📌 CORE CONCEPT

Function questions ask why a specific sentence, paragraph, or example exists in the passage — not what it says, but what role it plays. Understanding structure means recognizing how each piece supports, contrasts, or qualifies the overall argument.

How to Approach It
  • 1Identify what would be missing from the passage without that element — what argument or flow would break?
  • 2Consider how the element relates to surrounding content — does it support, contrast with, or qualify the main idea?
  • 3Choose the answer that describes the structural role, not a content summary of what the element says.
Example Question

Before standardized timekeeping, each British town kept local solar time, making railway schedules chaotic. Railway companies imposed a single standard time in 1847. Critics viewed this as an artificial imposition, yet within a generation standard time had become simply "time," its railway origins largely forgotten.

The final sentence ("its railway origins largely forgotten") primarily serves to:

(A) Criticize railway companies for not taking credit for their contribution (B) Suggest that standard time may eventually revert to local solar time (C) Illustrate how completely the reform was absorbed into everyday life (D) Warn that society should not repeat this pattern of institutional forgetting (E) Contrast nineteenth-century practices with those of modern society
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). The function of the phrase is to show how thoroughly the reform was accepted — so thoroughly that even its origin became invisible. This is a structural illustration of the main point about naturalization. (A), (B), (D), (E) introduce ideas not in the passage.

⚠️ COMMON TRAPS
  • Describing the content of the paragraph instead of its function — the correct answer describes a role like "to illustrate," "to contrast," "to qualify," not a summary of the paragraph's topic
  • Overstating the function — e.g., saying a counterexample "refutes" the argument when it only "qualifies" it
📋 LESSON SUMMARY
  • Function answers use active verbs: illustrate, contrast, qualify, support, introduce, concede
  • Ask: "What would be lost if this paragraph were removed?" — that's its function
  • The function is always in service of the author's overall argument
💡 QUICK TIP

The function is structural — "to illustrate," "to contrast," "to support" — not a content summary. Answer choices that begin with verbs describing purpose are almost always the right format.
