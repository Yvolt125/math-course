Lesson 1

Argument Structure

🎯 LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Identify the conclusion and premises of any GMAT Critical Reasoning argument
  • Recognise signal words that indicate conclusions vs. evidence
  • Articulate the logical gap between premise and conclusion
📌 CORE CONCEPT

Every CR argument has a conclusion — what the author is trying to prove — and premises — the evidence or facts offered in support. The gap between the premises and the conclusion is where the unstated assumption lives, and that gap is the target of most CR question types.

How to Approach It
  • 1Find the conclusion — it is often signaled by words like "therefore," "thus," "so," or "hence."
  • 2Identify the premises — the stated facts or evidence the author uses to support the conclusion.
  • 3Note the gap between premises and conclusion — that unstated bridge is the assumption the argument relies on.
Example Question

A new study shows that employees who work from home report higher job satisfaction than those who work in offices. The study's authors therefore conclude that companies should allow all employees to work from home.

Which of the following best identifies the conclusion of the argument?

(A) Employees who work from home are more productive than office workers (B) A new study examined job satisfaction among different types of workers (C) Companies should allow all employees to work from home (D) High job satisfaction leads to better business outcomes (E) Working from home causes higher job satisfaction
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). "Therefore conclude that companies should allow..." signals the conclusion. (B) is background context. (A) and (D) introduce claims not in the argument. (E) overstates — the study shows correlation, not causation.

⚠️ COMMON TRAPS
  • Confusing a premise (a stated fact) with the conclusion (what the author is trying to prove) — the conclusion follows FROM the premises, not the other way around
  • Treating background context as a premise — not everything stated in the argument is evidence for the conclusion
📋 LESSON SUMMARY
  • Conclusion signal words: therefore, thus, so, hence, consequently, this shows
  • Premise signal words: because, since, given that, as evidenced by
  • The gap between premises and conclusion is where the assumption lives — finding it is the key to most CR question types
💡 QUICK TIP

The conclusion is what the author is trying to prove, not a fact stated in the passage. If a statement could stand on its own as established evidence, it is a premise — not the conclusion.

Lesson 2

Strengthen & Weaken

🎯 LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Identify the core assumption of an argument
  • Select a strengthener that supports the assumption or eliminates an alternative explanation
  • Select a weakener that attacks the assumption or introduces an alternative explanation
📌 CORE CONCEPT

Strengthen questions ask for a choice that makes the conclusion more likely to be true — typically by supporting the argument's core assumption. Weaken questions ask for a choice that makes the conclusion less likely — typically by attacking the assumption or introducing counter-evidence. The conclusion itself does not change; only its probability does.

How to Approach It
  • 1Identify the conclusion and the central assumption before evaluating any answer choice.
  • 2For Strengthen: find a choice that supports the assumption, closes the gap, or adds new evidence pointing toward the conclusion.
  • 3For Weaken: find a choice that attacks the assumption, introduces a confounding factor, or provides counter-evidence against the conclusion.
Example Question

Northfield City plans to reduce traffic congestion by converting one lane of the main highway into a dedicated bus lane. City planners argue this will reduce total commute times because more commuters will switch to the bus.

Which of the following, if true, most weakens the city planners' argument?

(A) Bus fares in Northfield City are currently subsidised by the city government (B) Most Northfield commuters live too far from bus stops to use the bus service conveniently (C) Studies in other cities have shown that dedicated bus lanes improve bus journey times (D) The main highway is the only route connecting residential suburbs to the city centre (E) Traffic congestion in Northfield City has increased over the past decade
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (B). The planners assume commuters will switch to buses. If most cannot conveniently access bus stops, they won't switch — a lane is removed but car volume stays the same, worsening congestion. (A) doesn't affect whether people switch. (C) supports the plan. (D) is background. (E) is background.

⚠️ COMMON TRAPS
  • Choosing an answer that is about the same topic as the argument but does not actually affect the logic — relevance to the topic is not the same as impact on the conclusion
  • Confusing the direction: a weakener must make the conclusion less likely, not just introduce doubt about a premise
📋 LESSON SUMMARY
  • Strengthen: find a choice that makes the conclusion more probable — support the assumption or rule out alternatives
  • Weaken: find a choice that makes the conclusion less probable — attack the assumption or introduce an alternative cause
  • Correct answers affect the probability of the conclusion, they do not need to prove or disprove it absolutely
💡 QUICK TIP

The correct answer doesn't need to prove or disprove the conclusion outright — it just needs to shift the probability. Extreme answer choices that "prove" or "definitely disprove" are usually traps.

Lesson 3

Assumptions

🎯 LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Define the gap between stated premises and conclusion
  • Apply the Negation Test to verify a Necessary Assumption answer
  • Distinguish necessary assumptions (must be true for the argument to hold) from sufficient assumptions (alone would guarantee the conclusion)
📌 CORE CONCEPT

A necessary assumption is something that must be true for the argument to hold — remove it and the argument collapses. A sufficient assumption alone would guarantee the conclusion. Both types bridge the unstated gap between the premises and the conclusion, and neither is explicitly stated in the argument itself.

How to Approach It
  • 1Find the gap between the premise and the conclusion — this is where the assumption lives.
  • 2For Necessary Assumption: use the Negation Test — if negating the answer choice destroys the argument, that choice is a necessary assumption.
  • 3For Sufficient Assumption: look for a choice that, if added as a premise, makes the conclusion logically certain.
Example Question

Increasing the number of trees in urban areas reduces the urban heat island effect. Therefore, planting trees in downtown areas will reduce summer temperatures in those areas.

The argument assumes which of the following?

(A) The urban heat island effect is the primary cause of elevated summer temperatures (B) Downtown areas currently have fewer trees than they could support (C) Tree planting in downtown areas is less expensive than alternative cooling methods (D) Reducing the urban heat island effect will lower summer temperatures in those areas (E) Cities with more trees experience lower average annual temperatures
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (D). The argument moves from "trees reduce the heat island effect" to "temperatures will drop." This requires assuming that reducing the heat island effect actually lowers temperatures. Negation test: if reducing the heat island effect does NOT lower temperatures, the conclusion collapses. (A) introduces "primary cause" — not needed. (B) and (C) are irrelevant. (E) covers all temperatures, not just summer.

⚠️ COMMON TRAPS
  • Selecting an answer that is true and relevant but not actually required for the argument — pass the Negation Test: if negating the choice does NOT destroy the argument, it is not a necessary assumption
  • Choosing an assumption that is already stated in the argument — assumptions are always unstated
📋 LESSON SUMMARY
  • Negation Test: negate the answer choice. If the argument falls apart, it is a necessary assumption
  • Necessary assumptions bridge the gap — they are the minimum required for the logic to hold
  • Sufficient assumptions are stronger: they alone make the conclusion certain. Look for words like "if and only if" or absolute connections
💡 QUICK TIP

Assumptions are never stated in the argument — they bridge the unstated gap. If you see a choice that merely repeats something from the passage, it is not an assumption.

Lesson 4

Evaluate the Argument

🎯 LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Identify which factor, if known, would most help determine whether the argument's conclusion is valid
  • Recognise that evaluate answers are questions or variables, not statements of fact
  • Apply the "either way" test to confirm the correct answer
📌 CORE CONCEPT

Evaluate questions ask which piece of information would most help assess whether the argument's conclusion is valid. The answer is typically a factor or question whose answer could either confirm or undermine the argument's core assumption — it works in both directions.

How to Approach It
  • 1Identify the core assumption of the argument — this is the hinge point the evaluation will test.
  • 2Think of what evidence could either confirm or undermine that assumption before reading answer choices.
  • 3Choose the option that, answered in either direction (yes or no), would meaningfully affect the argument's validity.
Example Question

Museum attendance has fallen 30% over the past decade. To increase attendance, the director proposes adding an interactive digital exhibit, citing a survey showing that 60% of non-visitors said they would be more likely to visit if the museum had more interactive exhibits.

Which of the following would be most useful to know in evaluating the director's proposal?

(A) Whether the surveyed non-visitors have visited other museums with interactive exhibits (B) Whether interactive exhibits cost more to maintain than traditional exhibits (C) Whether the 60% who expressed interest are truly new potential visitors or current visitors who would visit more often (D) Whether the museum's staff have experience operating digital technology (E) Whether attendance declines were uniform across all visitor age groups
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (C). If the 60% are new visitors → the proposal could grow attendance (supports the plan). If they already visit regularly → the proposal won't grow total attendance (weakens the plan). This cuts both ways — exactly what an evaluate question requires. (B) is about cost, not attendance. (D) is irrelevant to whether attendance will rise.

⚠️ COMMON TRAPS
  • Selecting a factor that is interesting but would not actually change your assessment of the argument's strength — it must cut both ways (one answer strengthens, the other weakens)
  • Choosing a factor that is about a different aspect of the situation, not the core logical gap
📋 LESSON SUMMARY
  • The "either way" test: if knowing the answer to the evaluate choice could either strengthen or weaken the argument, it is the right type of answer
  • Evaluate questions require you to find the core assumption first — you are looking for what would test it
  • The correct answer is usually a question or measurable factor, not a conclusion
💡 QUICK TIP

The answer is usually a question or factor — not a statement of fact. Apply the "Yes/No Test": if answering yes strengthens the argument and answering no weakens it (or vice versa), it is the correct evaluator.

Lesson 5

Inference & Conclusion

🎯 LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • Identify what must be true given only the statements in the argument
  • Distinguish "must be true" from "likely to be true" or "could be true"
  • Avoid bringing in outside knowledge or assumptions when answering inference questions
📌 CORE CONCEPT

Inference questions ask what must be true based only on the statements given — no outside knowledge or assumptions allowed. The correct answer follows necessarily from the provided facts and closely mirrors the language and scope of the passage, making the smallest logical leap possible.

How to Approach It
  • 1Treat all given statements as facts — do not question their truth or introduce external knowledge.
  • 2Find a choice that is necessarily true given those facts — not just plausible or likely, but certain.
  • 3Eliminate choices that are merely possible, that go beyond the scope of the statements, or that require additional assumptions.
Example Question

Every employee who completed the advanced training programme received a pay raise last year. Marta received a pay raise last year but had not completed the advanced training programme.

Which of the following must be true based only on the statements above?

(A) Marta is the only employee who received a pay raise without completing the programme (B) Most employees who received pay raises had also completed the advanced training programme (C) Employees who complete the programme are more productive than those who do not (D) There is at least one way to receive a pay raise other than completing the training programme (E) The training programme is the most common reason employees receive pay raises
▶ Show Answer

Answer: (D). Marta received a raise without completing the programme, so at least one other path to a raise must exist. This is necessarily true. (A) adds "only" — not stated. (B) "most" is not supported. (C) introduces productivity — out of scope. (E) "most common" is not supported.

💡 QUICK TIP

Inference answers are conservative — they closely mirror the language of the passage and make minimal logical leaps. If an answer sounds dramatic or introduces new ideas, eliminate it immediately.
